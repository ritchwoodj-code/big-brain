# BIG BRAIN REVIEW

## Submission
A SaaS product that monitors competitors' pricing in real time and sends
alerts when prices change, allowing businesses to respond immediately.

## Routing
This is an idea validation decision (zero-to-one). The core question is
whether enough pain exists to justify building, whether the economics hold,
and whether the product can be positioned coherently. Invoking: Shark Tank,
Nasty Investor, Bezos, Jony Ive.

---

### SHARK TANK — Customer Clarity & Business Fundamentals
**Signal:** CONDITIONAL
**Key Insight:** The pain is real in e-commerce and retail, but "alert me when
a competitor changes price" is only valuable if the customer can actually act
on it. If their pricing process is manual or bureaucratic, the alert is noise.
**Hard Question:** What's the buyer's average time-to-response after getting a
price alert, and have you talked to anyone who's actually changed their price
within 24 hours of a competitor move?

---

### NASTY INVESTOR — Downside-First Capital
**Signal:** FAIL
**Key Insight:** Pricing intelligence tools are a crowded market with
established players (Prisync, Wiser, Skuuudle) who have years of indexed data
and direct retail partnerships. The moat question is unanswered: you're
describing a feature, not a company.
**Hard Question:** What stops one of those incumbents from shipping your exact
feature in a quarter, and what data asset do you accumulate that becomes
impossible to replicate?

---

### JEFF BEZOS — Customer Obsession & Mechanisms
**Signal:** CONDITIONAL
**Key Insight:** The PR/FAQ writes itself ("Retailers lose margin because they
react to competitor pricing too slowly") — that's a real customer problem.
But the mechanism is missing. What does the customer actually do after the
alert? The product needs to close the loop: detect change → recommend response
→ one-click reprice. Right now it stops at the notification.
**Hard Question:** If you wrote the PR/FAQ for this, would the FAQ section
include a question about why the customer shouldn't just do this with a free
scraper and a Slack webhook?

---

### JONY IVE — Subtractive Design Coherence
**Signal:** FAIL
**Key Insight:** "Alert dashboard with price change history" is not a product
idea, it's a feature spec. The emotional experience is nonexistent. The user
sits in front of a table of numbers, stressed. There's no coherent object here
— no moment of delight, no sense that a single designer thought about what
success feels like for the person using it.
**Hard Question:** What does the user feel the moment they open this product,
and is that feeling distinct from the anxiety of watching a spreadsheet?

---

## MEGA ROOM VERDICT
**Overall Signal:** YELLOW
**Primary Constraint:** This is a feature looking for a business. The pain is
real but the differentiation is not. Every persona is asking a version of the
same question: what happens after the alert? The product stops at the point
where the actual value would begin.
**Recommended Next Move:** Spend two weeks interviewing 10 e-commerce operators
who actively reprice. Find out how long it takes them to act on a competitor
price change and what stops them from acting faster. If the bottleneck is
information, your product is right. If the bottleneck is internal approval or
process, you're building the wrong thing entirely.

## NOTES ON THIS EXAMPLE

- Notice that three of four personas gave FAIL or CONDITIONAL, but the Mega
  Room Verdict was YELLOW not RED. That's because the underlying customer pain
  is confirmed — the gap is in the solution design, not the problem.

- The "Recommended Next Move" is always one action, not a roadmap.

- The Nasty Investor's FAIL does not kill the idea. It identifies the moat
  problem, which is solvable if addressed early.

- Jony Ive gave a FAIL on a pure business/data product. That is correct
  behavior. His lens applies. Even SaaS products have a UX experience that
  either creates or destroys user confidence.
