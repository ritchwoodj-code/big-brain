# BIG BRAIN — HOW TO READ A SIGNAL CORRECTLY
Big Brain by Polaris Digital Studio | polarisdgtl.com

This document explains how to use Big Brain output without misreading it.
The most common failure mode is not getting a bad review — it's
misinterpreting a good one.

---

## What the Signals Actually Mean

**GREEN** does not mean "this will succeed."
It means: the logic holds across the selected lenses and the primary
constraint is manageable given what was submitted. A GREEN on a vague
submission reflects the vagueness, not the opportunity.

**YELLOW** means: proceed, but the primary constraint identified in the
verdict must be resolved before you scale. Most good ideas start at YELLOW.
YELLOW is not a soft RED. It's the normal state of an honest plan.

**RED** means: the primary constraint is structural, not tactical. A RED
is not an instruction to quit — it's an instruction to redesign the premise
before investing more. The difference between a RED and a fatal outcome
is catching it here instead of six months in.

**CONDITIONAL** from any persona means that persona's hard question is a
blocking item, not a suggestion. A PASS from five personas and a CONDITIONAL
from one still requires the CONDITIONAL to be resolved. Don't average them.

## The Most Common Misread: The Confident GREEN

A submission that is clear, well-articulated, and enthusiastic will often
generate a GREEN. That's the correct mechanical output. But clarity is not
the same as correctness.

**Example of a misread GREEN:**

Submission: "I want to build a meal planning app that uses AI to generate
weekly menus based on your preferences and grocery budget."

Pre-flight answers:
- Decision: Whether to start building this month.
- 90-day success: 100 paying users.
- Most uncertain about: "Honestly not much — I've used every app in this
  space and they all suck. I know what's missing."

The pre-flight answer to question 3 is a red flag. "Not much uncertain" in
a crowded consumer market is almost never true — it usually means the person
hasn't stress-tested their assumptions yet. A Big Brain review run on this
submission would likely generate CONDITIONAL signals from Nasty Investor
(on unit economics and market saturation) and Shark Tank (on why this
founder specifically has the edge), but the overall routing would still
trend YELLOW-to-GREEN if the idea is articulated well.

The misread: the founder sees GREEN/YELLOW and starts building.

**What should have happened:** The Nasty Investor's hard question — "What
stops Instacart, Kroger, or a $5M-funded competitor from shipping this
feature in a quarter?" — should have been treated as a blocking CONDITIONAL,
not skimmed past. The primary constraint was competitive moat, and it was
never resolved in the submission.

**The rule:** A GREEN with unresolved CONDITIONALs is a YELLOW. Always
resolve every CONDITIONAL before treating the verdict as actionable.

---

## The Thing You Were Defensive About Is the Review That Matters

Pre-flight question 3 exists for one reason: the thing you're most defensive
about is almost always the weakest part of the plan. Not because you're
dishonest — because you've already argued yourself into a position on it,
and your internal narrative has calcified.

If the Big Brain review didn't challenge the thing you flagged in question 3,
it means either:
(a) the selected personas genuinely don't have a strong lens on that issue, or
(b) your framing of the submission pre-answered the question before the review ran

If (a), manually run the persona most likely to challenge it — usually
Nasty Investor or Musk — as a single-persona review focused only on that question.

If (b), resubmit with the uncertain thing stated plainly rather than embedded
in the framing. "I'm not sure if the pricing model holds at scale" is better
input than a submission that subtly justifies the pricing model in its setup.

## How to Use a RED Correctly

A RED verdict is only useful if you treat it as a design problem, not a
verdict on your worth as a founder or builder.

The correct sequence after a RED:
1. Read the Primary Constraint. That's the root issue.
2. Read every FAIL and CONDITIONAL. They're symptoms of the same root.
3. Ask: can the premise be redesigned to address the primary constraint?
4. If yes, redesign and resubmit. A good redesign often takes 20 minutes.
5. If no, the constraint is fundamental. The timing, market, or model is wrong.
   Move resources elsewhere.

The wrong sequence after a RED:
1. Decide the review missed something important.
2. Select only the personas that gave PASS signals and call that the real verdict.
3. Proceed anyway.

Big Brain cannot stop motivated reasoning. It can only surface the constraint
clearly enough that ignoring it requires a deliberate choice.

---

## When to Resubmit

Resubmit when:
- You resolved the primary constraint and want to verify the new approach
- Your pre-flight answers were vague and you can now answer more specifically
- More than 30 days have passed and the context has changed materially
- The submission was an idea and it's now an execution plan (different decision type)

Don't resubmit to get a better signal on the same unchanged plan.

---

## The Limitation Big Brain Won't Tell You

The review is only as good as what you submit. Big Brain has no access to
your market, your competitors, your team, your customers, or your financials
unless you put them in the submission. A persona that would give a FAIL if
it knew your true CAC will give a CONDITIONAL or even PASS if it only knows
your projected CAC.

The signal is a function of the input. The quality of the input is on you.
